Starting Out

From an early age, sport was a constant in my life. I cannot remember a time when I was not competing in some form, always driven by a natural enjoyment of movement and competition.

I met my first coach, Phil Budd, who identified my strengths early and helped establish a strong technical and physical foundation. In January 2019, after just one month of structured training, I placed third at the County Championships as a bottom-year athlete.

At an age where physical maturity often dictates success, this performance challenged expectations and marked a turning point.

By the age of ten, I moved into a specialised middle-distance training group under my second coach, Alan Crook.

The Competition

Throughout my running career, I have encountered numerous challenges through formidable teammates and rivals. My first significant rival was Eddie Clegg, whose constant presence pushed my early development.

He possessed an intense and highly competitive running style that tested my physical and mental limits. Adapting to his unorthodox approach taught me how to navigate high-pressure scenarios and ultimately made me a more resilient athlete.

Another significant influence on my career is Max Harrison, a runner for Hercules Wimbledon. We first met at Young Athlete Camp, where we developed a close friendship.

Despite our contrasting running styles, we constantly challenge one another during training. Max has instilled in me a deep sense of drive and a professional standard of discipline; because of his influence, my commitment is unwavering, and skipping sessions is no longer a consideration.

Finally, I have a strong competitive relationship with Luca Di Giovanni. Although he is a year older and trains in a different group within our club, we maintain a healthy rivalry.

Training & Development

My training is structured around long-term athletic development rather than short-term success. Instead of chasing volume for its own sake, I keep my weekly load controlled at around 35 km.

A typical week includes 2 quality sessions, 3 easy runs, 1 cross-training session, and 2 gym sessions. This helps recovery, supports consistency, and improves power output through better strength and mechanics.

Through summer, the emphasis shifts toward speed work and shorter repetitions to sharpen race-specific performance.
